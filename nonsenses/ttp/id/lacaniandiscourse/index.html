Lacanian-Discourse_01

Discourse：

• Agent：The subject that initiates discourse, the speaking subject.

• Other：The addressee of discourse (the recipient / the disciplined subject).

• Truth：The foundational truth or driving force concealed behind discourse.

• Production：The surplus, result, or alienated product generated by discourse.

(Note: Some translations render Truth as “driving force/truth” and Other as “the other/production,” with limited accuracy.)

The Structural Positions of Discourse（Syntax-like Position）

Agent → Other

Truth → Production

  1. Master’s Discourse（Discourse du maître）

Agent → Other S1 → S2

Truth→ Production $ a

Example:
The modern state does not conduct ideological operations directly in the name of “the government.” Instead, it operates through “narrating nationhood,” using the government-controlled subject-position as a proxy for “the right to speak for the nation,” thereby producing S1. It never directly declares “The People’s Republic of China demands your patriotism,” but rather speaks in formulations like “The Chinese nation cannot be bullied anymore.” In this scenario, the government is not the direct speaking signifier, but rather occupies the position of proxying “national narrative,” thus constituting the master signifier (S1).

• S1：The position from which the government speaks “in the name of the nation”—the signifying structure of narrating nationhood.

• S2：Ideological content, such as national sentiment, narratives of sacrifice, discourse of suffering and revival.

• $：The masses named as “members of the nation,” summoned as an anonymous collective.

• a：The heretic, the excluded other.

The key point is that all attempts at totalization are destined to fail.
The master’s discourse conceals the split of the subject.

  1. University Discourse（Discourse de l’université）

Agent → Other S2 → a

Truth → Production S1→ $

Official statement example:

“The Department recognizes that for the graduate student, the educational and financial benefits of teaching eventually are offset by its time demands.”

• S2：The subject initiating discourse (le savoir / knowledge as the product of discursive structure), such as: “The Department recognizes teaching is beneficial / we recognize trade-offs / you grow through labor.”

• S1：The foundational truth hidden behind discourse (the master signifier)—namely, “exploitation.”

• a：The addressee—the repressed graduate student.

• $：The surplus or alienated product generated by discourse—“the student’s weariness toward teaching duties.”

The visible layer of discourse is the Agent’s address to the Other (S2 → a),
but what truly drives the entire discursive system is the S1 (master signifier) in the Truth position.
The alienated result produced by this address is $ (production).

Appendix: The Operating Paradigm of Master’s Discourse in Gender and Desire Order

Mainstream discourse (especially heteronormative frameworks) often forcibly defines the desire-position of the other, forming discursive hegemony.
This hegemony is particularly evident in the symbol of the Phallus. The Phallus is not a biological signifier, but rather the symbolic signifier of desire, constituting the nodal point through which the subject enters the symbolic order.

In discourse structures dominated by heterosexual regimes, the position of the Phallus is naturalized as the template for “universal desire,” thereby excluding the desire-experiences of marginal subjects from the realm of linguistic expressibility.
When mainstream discourse attempts to fit the emotions or desires of sexual minorities into this “phallocentric” framework, it not only causes symbolic misalignment but also materially deprives marginal subjects of the power to construct their own language of desire.
This symbolic hegemony, through normative discourse, makes mainstream symbolic structures appear “natural,” thereby degrading marginal desires to the status of deviation.
